Sudoku Zero-Knowledge Proof

Sudoku solutions can be proven via zero-knowledge proof. If Peggy has a solution to Victor's puzzle, she can convince him her solution is valid without ever revealing the solution — or any information about it beyond its existence.

The Setup

Two parties:

  • Peggy (Prover) — has a valid solution to a Sudoku puzzle.
  • Victor (Verifier) — has the puzzle and wants proof the solution exists, but Peggy won't reveal it without payment.

The Protocol

The Sudoku ZKP works very similarly to the graph coloring proof. Because every valid Sudoku solution remains valid under any relabeling of the digits 1–9, Peggy can hide her solution simply by applying a random digit permutation $\sigma : \{1,\ldots,9\} \to \{1,\ldots,9\}$ before committing.

Step 1 — Commit

Peggy permutes her solution with a freshly chosen $\sigma$ and then commits to every cell using a cryptographic hash with a random nonce:

$$\text{commit}_{i} = \text{SHA-256}\!\left(\,\sigma(d_i) \;\|\; r_i\,\right)$$

where $d_i$ is the original digit at cell $i$ and $r_i$ is a large random string chosen fresh every round. She sends all 81 commitments to Victor. Victor sees 81 sealed envelopes — he cannot open any of them.

Step 2 — Challenge

Victor randomly picks one of 27 challenge groups: any of the 9 rows, 9 columns, or 9 subsquares. He may also ask Peggy to reveal all preset cells to verify permutation consistency. Each group must contain digits 1–9 exactly once in any valid solution.

Step 3 — Reveal & Verify

Peggy opens the 9 commitments for the challenged group, disclosing the permuted digit and nonce for each. Victor verifies three things:

  • Every hash matches: $\text{SHA-256}(\sigma(d_i) \| r_i) = \text{commit}_i$
  • The 9 revealed digits are all distinct (1–9, each exactly once)
  • For preset cells: the permutation $\sigma$ is consistent across the board

If Peggy is cheating, at least one row, column, or subsquare contains a duplicate. The probability of Victor missing it in a single round is at most $\tfrac{26}{27}$. After $k$ independent rounds:

$$P(\text{not caught after } k \text{ rounds}) \;\leq\; \left(\frac{26}{27}\right)^{\!k}$$
Why This Is Zero-Knowledge
Every round uses a fresh random permutation. The 9 digits Victor sees are just some arrangement of 1–9 — information he already knew any valid group must contain. He learns nothing about the actual digit assignment. A simulator could produce identically distributed transcripts without ever seeing the real solution.

How Many Rounds?

With 27 possible challenge groups, the soundness error per round is $\frac{26}{27} \approx 96.3\%$. This decreases exponentially with each round:

Rounds ($k$) Soundness error $\left(\tfrac{26}{27}\right)^k$ Verifier confidence

Compare with graph coloring, which had $\frac{m-1}{m}$ soundness error per round where $m$ is the number of edges. For the classic 6-edge example that was $\frac{5}{6} \approx 83.3\%$ per round — much better per-round guarantees. Sudoku's 27 groups give weaker per-round reduction but the protocol applies to a richer, more structured problem.
